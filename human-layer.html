Responsible AI

Anticipating and guiding human-AI interaction at the level of the individual, the group, and the population.

The hard part of AI is human.

Most of the difficulty in deploying AI well is not in the model. It is in the layer where models meet people, teams, and institutions. Capabilities are improving faster than our collective ability to absorb them, and what determines whether a system helps or harms is rarely the architecture. It is adoption dynamics, trust calibration, incentive structures, and the social context the system enters. That human layer is where I work.

My background sits deliberately across four fields that rarely share a table: data science, psychology, organizational psychology, and applied ethics. That combination lets me reason about AI systems and the people who use them in the same breath, rather than treating the technical and the human as separate problems.

A focus on bias and fairness

Bias is the throughline of my work in AI, because it is where the technical and the human are hardest to pull apart. A model learns the patterns in its training data, including the historical inequities baked into that data, and then reproduces them at scale and at speed. The result can be a system that looks neutral and rigorous while quietly encoding the very disparities it was meant to remove.

I treat bias as a measurement problem first. Drawing on a psychometrician's discipline, I look at construct validity (is the model measuring what we think it is measuring), at label and sampling bias in the underlying data, and at how a single fairness number can hide very different outcomes across groups. Fairness is not one quantity but several that often trade off against each other, so the honest work is naming which definition a system is optimizing for and who bears the cost when definitions conflict.

Just as important is the bias that lives in people, not models. Automation bias leads users to over-trust a confident output; confirmation bias leads them to accept the results that match their priors and scrutinize the ones that do not. Algorithmic bias and human bias compound each other, and a fairness audit that ignores the human side will miss most of the harm.

From the individual to the population

Human-AI interaction has to be understood at more than one scale, because effects that look benign for one person can compound dangerously across many. This is fundamentally a systems-thinking problem: the behavior that matters emerges from feedback loops between people, models, and institutions, not from any single component in isolation.

  • Individual. How a single user calibrates trust, interprets model outputs, and adjusts their own judgment. This is the micro-level loop of human and machine, and the level at which automation bias first takes hold.
  • Group. How AI reshapes collaboration, deference, and decision rights. Shared reliance on one system can quietly narrow the range of views a group actually considers, and can launder a biased recommendation into a consensus no one quite chose.
  • Population. The emergent, second-order effects. Feedback loops between recommender systems and behavior, homogenization of judgment at scale, and the polarization and trust dynamics that surface only when millions of interactions aggregate. Biased systems are most dangerous here, where small per-decision disparities accumulate into structural ones. My research on depolarization and collective behavior speaks directly to this level.

Why this background anticipates what's coming

Predicting how people will respond to a new technology is, fundamentally, a behavioral science question, and behavioral science at scale is a data problem. I am trained to do both: to design studies that capture how humans actually behave around AI, and to model those patterns quantitatively so organizations can plan for them before they become incidents. The goal is foresight, not reaction. That means identifying where bias, over-reliance, eroded trust, or unintended social effects are likely to emerge, and building guardrails and governance that fit how people genuinely behave.

Engagements include

  • AI bias and fairness audits across data, models, and the humans in the loop
  • Human-factors and adoption review for AI products and internal tools
  • Trust, reliance, and over-automation risk assessment
  • Validation and bias auditing of model and human-performance metrics
  • AI governance and responsible-deployment frameworks
  • Research on population-level effects of AI-mediated interaction
  • Advisory and education for leadership navigating AI adoption

Stance

I am neither an AI booster nor a doomer. I take these systems seriously as genuinely powerful tools and as genuine sources of risk, and I believe the most important work right now is unglamorous: measuring bias carefully, anticipating human responses honestly, thinking in systems rather than parts, and designing the human layer with as much rigor as we design the models.
