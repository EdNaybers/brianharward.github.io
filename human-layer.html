The Human Layer

Anticipating and guiding human–AI interaction — at the level of the individual, the team, and the population.

The hard part of AI is human.

Most of the difficulty in deploying AI well is not in the model — it is in the layer where models meet people, teams, and institutions. Capabilities are improving faster than our collective ability to absorb them. What determines whether a system helps or harms is rarely the architecture; it is adoption dynamics, trust calibration, incentive structures, and the social context the system enters. That layer — the human one — is where I work.

My background sits deliberately across four fields that rarely share a table: data science, psychology, organizational psychology, and applied ethics. That combination lets me reason about AI systems and the people who use them in the same breath, rather than treating the technical and the human as separate problems.

What I bring

  • Measurement that holds up. A psychometrician's discipline applied to AI: construct validity, reliability, and bias auditing for the metrics teams use to judge models and the humans who work alongside them. If you cannot measure it well, you cannot govern it.
  • Human factors of AI use. Trust calibration, automation bias, over-reliance and under-reliance, and the cognitive offloading that reshapes how people think when a capable system is in the loop.
  • Organizational adoption. How AI changes roles, norms, and accountability inside real organizations — and how to design rollouts that account for behavior, not just workflow diagrams.
  • Ethics with operational teeth. Pluralistic, practical ethical analysis that survives contact with product deadlines, rather than a values statement no one reads.

From the individual to the population

Human–AI interaction has to be understood at more than one scale, because effects that look benign for one person can compound dangerously across many.

  • Individual. How a single user calibrates trust, interprets model outputs, and adjusts their own judgment — the micro-level loop of human and machine.
  • Group and team. How AI reshapes collaboration, deference, and decision rights; how shared reliance on a system can quietly narrow the range of views a team actually considers.
  • Population. The emergent, second-order effects: feedback loops between recommender systems and behavior, homogenization of judgment at scale, and the polarization and trust dynamics that surface only when millions of interactions aggregate. My research on depolarization and collective behavior is directly relevant here.

Why this background anticipates what's coming

Predicting how people will respond to a new technology is, fundamentally, a behavioral science question — and behavioral science at scale is a data problem. I am trained to do both: to design studies that capture how humans actually behave around AI, and to model those patterns quantitatively so organizations can plan for them before they become incidents. The goal is foresight, not reaction: identifying where over-reliance, eroded trust, or unintended social effects are likely to emerge, and building guardrails and governance that fit how people genuinely behave.

Engagements include

  • Human-factors and adoption review for AI products and internal tools
  • Trust, reliance, and over-automation risk assessment
  • Validation and bias auditing of model and human-performance metrics
  • AI governance and responsible-deployment frameworks
  • Research on population-level effects of AI-mediated interaction
  • Advisory and education for leadership navigating AI adoption

Stance

I am neither an AI booster nor a doomer. I take these systems seriously as genuinely powerful tools and as genuine sources of risk, and I believe the most important work right now is unglamorous: measuring carefully, anticipating human responses honestly, and designing the human layer with as much rigor as we design the models.
